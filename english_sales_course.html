Practice dialogues — annotated cold calls

Scenario: SaaS software sales — outbound cold call
You are Serkan, a sales rep at a productivity software company. You're calling Sarah Mitchell, Operations Manager at a mid-sized logistics firm. She wasn't expecting your call.
Moderate
Phase 1 — Opening
SR
"Hi, is this Sarah Mitchell?" confirm name
Always confirm the name first — it shows professionalism and avoids the wrong conversation. Say it as a question, not a statement.
SM
"Yes, speaking. Who's this?"
SR
"Hi Sarah, my name is Serkan — I'm calling from Flowdesk. I know you weren't expecting my call, so I'll keep it brief." disarm opener
"I'll keep it brief" is a powerful disarm — it respects their time and reduces the urge to hang up. Use this phrase every time.
Turkish tip: avoid "I am disturbing you" — this sounds overly apologetic in English sales. "I know you weren't expecting my call" is the natural equivalent.
Phase 2 — Hook
SM
"Alright... what's it about?"
SR
"We work with logistics companies to reduce the time their teams spend on manual reporting — typically saving around four to six hours a week per team. I just wanted to find out if that's even a challenge for you right now." value + question
Lead with a specific benefit, then ask a soft question. Don't pitch your product — pitch the problem you solve. "I just wanted to find out if..." sounds curious, not pushy.
Phase 3 — Objection
SM
"Honestly, we already use a system for that. I'm not sure we're looking to change anything."
SR
"That makes total sense — most of the teams I speak to are already using something. Can I ask, are you happy with how it's working, or are there still a few pain points?" reframe
"That makes total sense" validates their objection without agreeing with it. Then you pivot with a question. Never argue — redirect.
Vocabulary: "pain points" = the problems your customer experiences. Very common in sales English. "A few pain points" sounds softer than "problems."
Phase 4 — Discovery
SM
"Well... the reporting side is okay, but our team still has to export everything manually into Excel. It takes forever."
SR
"That's actually exactly what we help with — automating that export process so it just happens in the background. Would it be worth a quick twenty-minute call this week to show you how it works? No commitment, just a look." soft close
"No commitment, just a look" removes pressure and makes it easy to say yes. The goal of a cold call is NOT to sell — it's to book the next conversation.
Phase 5 — Close
SM
"Okay... sure, I could do Thursday afternoon."
SR
"Perfect. Thursday afternoon works great. I'll send a calendar invite to your work email — is it sarah.mitchell at the same company domain?" confirm details
Always confirm logistics before hanging up. Repeat back the day and time. Asking for their email keeps you in control of the next step.
Vocabulary: "calendar invite" and "domain" are standard B2B terms. Practice saying them naturally — Turkish learners often over-stress the second syllable in "domain."

SR
Scenario: B2B recruitment software — inbound follow-up call
You are Serkan, a sales rep at a hiring software company. James Okafor, Head of HR at a manufacturing firm, filled in a contact form two weeks ago but never replied to your follow-up email. He's busy, skeptical, and used to being sold to.
Hard prospect
Phase 1 — Opening (cold re-contact)
SR
"Hi James, this is Serkan from HireFlow. You filled in a form on our website a couple of weeks ago — I just wanted to make sure it didn't get lost." soft opener
"I just wanted to make sure it didn't get lost" is low-pressure. It implies you're being helpful, not chasing. Much better than "I'm calling to follow up on my email."
JO
"Oh. Yeah, I saw your email. Honestly I've been slammed. We're probably not the right fit anyway — we already use something."
Two objections at once — "too busy" AND "already have a solution." Don't try to answer both. Pick one and stay calm.
Phase 2 — Handling the brush-off
SR
"Totally understand — sounds like a hectic period. I'm not going to pretend this is urgent on your end. Can I just ask one quick question before I let you go?" permission ask
"Before I let you go" signals you respect their time and won't talk for 20 minutes. It also makes them curious what the question is. Most people say yes.
JO
"...Fine. Make it quick."
SR
"When you filled in the form — what were you actually trying to solve at the time?" intent question
This is your best question. It goes back to their original intent — before they got busy. "At the time" invites them to remember the problem that made them search for you in the first place.
Phase 3 — Uncovering the real problem
JO
"We were struggling to get decent applicants for our floor supervisors. Our current system is fine for office roles but it's terrible for shift work scheduling and filtering."
SR
"That's actually a really common gap — most general HR platforms weren't built with shift-based roles in mind. Has that problem gone away, or is it still there in the background?" reframe + probe
"Still there in the background" acknowledges they've been busy, but plants the idea that the problem hasn't been solved — just deprioritised. This reopens urgency without pressure.
Vocabulary: "in the background" = a problem that exists but isn't being actively dealt with. Very natural in English business conversation.
Phase 4 — Budget objection
JO
"It's still there. But look — budget is tight this quarter. I can't justify a new platform right now."
SR
"That's fair — I'm not here to push anything on a tight quarter. Would it make sense to at least see the numbers so you have something ready when budget opens up? Takes thirty minutes." future pacing
"Future pacing" = moving the conversation to a future moment when the obstacle no longer exists. You're not asking them to buy — you're asking them to be ready to buy. This removes pressure completely.
Turkish tip: avoid "I understand your problem" — in English this can sound patronising. Say "That's fair" or "That makes sense" — shorter and warmer.
Phase 5 — Soft close
JO
"I mean... yeah. If it's just thirty minutes, I suppose I could do next week."
SR
"Perfect. I'll find a slot and send it over — Tuesday or Wednesday work better for you?" either/or close
The either/or close offers two specific options instead of asking "when are you free?" — which is easy to ignore. It moves the conversation forward without feeling pushy.
JO
"Wednesday afternoon is usually better."
SR
"Wednesday afternoon it is. I'll send a calendar link in the next hour. And James — thanks for giving me the minute. I know you were slammed." graceful exit
Ending by acknowledging their time ("I know you were slammed") leaves a positive impression. It shows self-awareness and respect. Small phrase, big impact on how they remember the call.
